# GET IT DONE — Felmark's Product Philosophy

> Three words. One philosophy. Every screen follows it.

---

## The Three Words

**GET** — What do I need right now? Which client? Which project? Which invoice? The Workspace surfaces the answer before you ask the question. You open Felmark and it says: "Bolt Fitness owes you $4,000 and it's 4 days late. Nora signed the contract 1 hour ago. Luna submitted an inquiry." You didn't search for this. You didn't click through menus. It's there. GET is the Workspace knowing what you need and putting it in front of you.

**IT** — What is the thing? Not an abstract status or a dashboard metric. The actual artifact. The invoice. The proposal. The contract. The message. IT is always concrete, always actionable, always one click away from being in your hands or in the client's hands. The Workspace doesn't show you "3 pending invoices" as a number — it shows you the three invoices, who they're for, how much, and a button to send each one.

**DONE** — The action. Send the invoice. Sign the contract. Reply to the message. Log the hours. Move the deal. Collect the payment. DONE is the verb. Every element in the Workspace has a verb attached to it. Nothing is just display. Everything is "here's the thing, here's what you do with it, do it now."

---

## How This Shapes Every Screen

### The Homepage — A To-Do List That Writes Itself

Not a dashboard. Not charts and metrics. The Workspace opens with: "Here's what needs your attention right now." Three sections, always in this order:

**GET** — What's urgent. Overdue invoices. Unsigned contracts. Unread client messages. Projects past deadline. These aren't buried in a sidebar. They're the first thing you see. Red borders. Action buttons. "Invoice overdue — Bolt Fitness — $4,000 — Send Reminder." One line. One button. Done.

**IT** — What's in progress. Active projects with their current state. Proposals waiting for signatures. Invoices that have been sent but not paid. Things that are moving but need monitoring. Amber indicators. "Proposal viewed 3 times — Meridian — not yet signed — Follow up." The Workspace tells you what each thing IS right now.

**DONE** — What's been completed. Payments received today. Contracts signed this week. Projects delivered. Green checkmarks. This isn't just a feel-good section — it's proof of momentum. You see $1,800 came in this morning and you think: this is working.

---

### The Client Card

Open a client card and you don't see a profile page. You see:

**GET** — What does this client need from me right now? "Brand Guidelines: client left feedback 2 days ago, you haven't responded." "Invoice #47: sent 15 days ago, not paid, due date was yesterday."

**IT** — What are the active things? The project at 67%. The proposal they're reviewing. The last 3 messages. The money: earned vs owed.

**DONE** — What can I do right this second? "Reply to feedback." "Send payment reminder." "Create next invoice." "Schedule a call." Buttons. Not links to other pages. Buttons that do the thing.

---

### The Pipeline

**GET** — Which deals need movement? Cards that have been stuck in the same column for too long get a visual nudge. "Luna Boutique has been in Lead for 5 days. Send a proposal?"

**IT** — What's each deal? The card shows the client, the value, the last activity, and the next step. Not just a name in a column.

**DONE** — Drag it. Move the card to the next stage. That action triggers downstream automation through the Forge: moving to "Proposal Sent" auto-generates the send date. Moving to "Active" creates the first invoice. Moving to "Complete" triggers the final invoice and archives the project.

---

### Invoicing

**GET** — Who hasn't paid? Sorted by urgency. Most overdue at the top.

**IT** — The invoice itself. Amount, date, status, payment link, view count.

**DONE** — "Send reminder." "Mark as paid." "Create new invoice." Three buttons per invoice. The freelancer doesn't navigate to an invoice detail page, edit fields, and click save. They click one button and it's done.

## Why This Kills HoneyBook

HoneyBook's UI is organized by feature: "Invoices" tab, "Proposals" tab, "Contracts" tab, "Projects" tab. That's a filing cabinet. You have to know which drawer to open.

Felmark's Workspace is organized by action: "What needs doing" → "What you can do" → "What's been done." The filing cabinet is invisible. The Forge handles the categorization. The user just sees: here's the next thing, do it.

A wedding photographer finishes a shoot. She opens Felmark. The Workspace says: "GET: Sarah & Mike's album is due in 3 days. IT: Album delivery project at 80%. DONE: Upload final files → Send delivery link → Create final invoice." She doesn't navigate. She doesn't think about which feature to use. She follows the flow.

---

## Workspace vs Workstation

**The Workspace** is the doer. GET IT DONE. Execution. Moving things forward. Every item has a verb.

**The Workstation** is the maker. Creation. A custom proposal. A detailed contract. A creative brief. Different mode. Different interface. Same data.

The Workspace handles everything about action. The Workstation handles everything about creation. Most days, the doer is all you need.

---

## The Standard

Every screen, every component, every block should pass this test:

1. **Does it show what needs attention?** (GET)
2. **Does it show the actual thing, not just a metric?** (IT)
3. **Does it have a verb — something the user can do right now?** (DONE)

If the answer to any of these is no, the screen isn't finished.
